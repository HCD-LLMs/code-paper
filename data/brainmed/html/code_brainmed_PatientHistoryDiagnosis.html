Medical Notes
The Grad-CAM analysis of the 3D MRI scan highlights several regions that are strongly associated with neurodegenerative changes typically seen in Alzheimer’s disease. Most notably, there is clear evidence of temporal lobe atrophy, particularly affecting the medial temporal structures, such as the hippocampus and entorhinal cortex. These regions are among the earliest to show signs of degeneration in Alzheimer’s and are closely linked to the progressive memory loss that characterizes the disease. In addition, the scan reveals cortical thinning in the parietal lobes, with a focus on the posterior parietal cortex. This area plays a key role in visuospatial processing and attention, and its involvement further supports the diagnosis of an Alzheimer’s-type process.

Another significant finding is the presence of subcortical white matter hyperintensities, which appear as bright areas on the scan. These changes may reflect underlying small vessel disease or demyelination and can contribute to cognitive impairment, especially when co-existing with Alzheimer’s pathology.

Axial, coronal, and sagittal slices confirm these observations, showing focal gray matter loss and disruption in white matter integrity across several cortical and subcortical structures. These changes point to a widespread pattern of neurodegeneration, affecting both gray and white matter.

Taken together, the imaging features identified by the model are highly consistent with a diagnosis of Alzheimer’s disease, or at least an Alzheimer’s-dominant neurodegenerative process. Clinical correlation is recommended, particularly with respect to cognitive symptoms and functional decline. Additional biomarker studies—such as CSF analysis or amyloid-PET imaging—may further support the diagnosis.
